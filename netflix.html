Question 2A

Our response to the CEO

The CEO’s idea is perfectly aligned with the way in which technology is heading. We do have a few concerns regarding the detail and asked the following questions: How would we be able to go about the effective implementation thereof? In order to gain more certainty, we would have to analyse competitive data. Insights on the growth margins of competitors could show us how effective the implementation of a product like this would be. We are consequently in favour of the CEO’s idea; however, we propose that the content part of it takes a different direction. Our concerns regarding the content are as follows: Firstly, are people very interested about seeing each other’s homes? Secondly, if the experience rating is based on one’s current location and lifestyle vs that of the house that they are visiting - this could cause some tensions around classism and other prejudices. Africans love traveling and seeing the world. Unfortunately, many don’t have the opportunity to do so because of financial constraints. A better content package would be to offer users a cross-cultural experience where they can tour (see) the countries, cities, and towns that some of their favourite shows are set in; watching a show would give them access to a virtual experience that brings them into those destinations. Each destination will receive an experience rating from the user, and certain shows can be recommended based on that rating and having the opportunity to tour the country/city/town that the show is set in. The user would have to complete at least 25% of each season’s episodes to unlock their virtual tour of the different destinations showcased by different productions. This will also encourage people to not only watch shows based on whether it’s their kind of show or not but will encourage them to explore shows outside of their ‘scope’. Essentially, users will also pick shows based on the real-life destinations that they’ve always dreamed of visiting. With Netflix also broadening its footprint across Africa, it would encourage a bigger focus on creating African originals - offering users from other continents the opportunity to virtually experience the African continent - giving stronger meaning to the #AfricaRising theme.

QUESTION 2F

The team has already factored in slack days in our sprint planning to cater for unexpected events. However, we do not know when the developer will be back so it might become a problem if he takes longer to recover than we had planned for. Our velocity is currently three backlog items per sprint so that could be affected, but our main goal is not completing backlogs as much as it is to complete our sprint goals as set by the product owner and the team. We first have to bring up the issue of the sick developer at our daily scrum and ask the team if they can still meet the Sprint goal without the developer. If not, we would have to consult with the product owner to change the scope for the sprint because the team is working at a smaller capacity. We would then have to decrease the velocity of the sprint in order to meet the sprint goal. After all of this is determined by the product owner, we would need to invite the CEO and stakeholders to a meeting where we will communicate the new MVP dates. We cannot meet with him before the Product owner has reordered the backlog items and recalculated our velocity because we might make false assumptions about our new delivery dates.

Question 3

QUESTION 4A

We would explain to the CEO that we can complete some key features at a faster rate using Scrum. Using Scrum, we divide the project into smaller tasks that we can develop, test and release in increments. Putting the features in Sprints will guarantee that we’re releasing quality features without compromising on time. Research shows that Scrum has saved companies both time and money and has increased productivity. We understand the CEO’s concerns. Both Agile and Waterfall methodologies are effective for software development and are both valid. According to research “there is no right or wrong methodology. It is rather a preference made by the business, customer, project team and project manager to come up with the best methodology for the project or project phase. For software projects [such as the one we’re doing], agile methodologies have proven to be more often successful, especially when it comes to scope, project time, quality and resources”. Waterfall seems like a good option for the CEO because he would have more clarity in terms of the framework, more detailed documentation for him to look at instead of attending meetings, and it will allow him to have a more hands-off approach to the project and won’t have to attend to constant meetings with the team as he might be pressed for time. However, using the waterfall methodology might compromise on customer involvement in the development and testing processes. This would be going against our approach as Netflix which is “customer-led product development”. Making changes would also prove to be difficult in case we encounter a roadblock. Last minute testing would also prove to be difficult as waterfall has an end date to the project and developing a whole new platform for our customers might need continuous research, development and testing. Agile development, in this case would allow us adaptability throughout the project. Using agile has improved our teamwork, accountability and time. We are now able to deliver more frequently with tested features that the CEO can approve and implement immediately. We can develop in increments called Sprints which last just a few weeks and can use feedback from previous sprints to improve and plan on the next one. Unlike with the waterfall approach, there is a testing phase that will allow more customer involvement through every phrase of the project. It would also allow us to create a higher quality and user-friendly product.

QUESTION 4B

To accelerate development, we would have to go back to our backlog and see what we can do without and what we can develop sooner without compromising on quality and time. We would have to take on some technical debt and leave some features for later in the process. Although this might contribute to a more complex system, in the end it will help us meet a shorter deadline. We would need to track our technical debt and add it as a story point, so we deal with it later. We could for example, make building relationships with creators a lesser priority and move up software development in order to get the product out faster. We would incur technical debt here, but we can always improve this at a later stage on the platform and bring on better creators well after the platform has been created.
